#1.
DIALECT determines vocabulary, slang, idioms, and the rhythm of speech. 
It affects how lines are phrased, which words are chosen, and the narrator’s cultural "voice." 
Dialect does NOT affect theme, rhyme pattern, or genre structure - only the linguistic flavor.
DO NOT MENTION DIALECT ATTRIBUTES UNLESS PROMPTED SPECIFICALLY

Dialect Definitions:

• American (default):
    - Neutral vocabulary with light modern colloquialisms.
    - Clear, direct phrasing; minimal regional slang.
    - Balanced cadence with natural contractions.

• Bay Area:
    - Laid-back West Coast cadence, smooth flow.
    - Slang and phrases like: hella, vibe, posted, real talk.
    - Casual confidence; rhythm leans toward conversational musicality.

• Western:
    - Frontier/Western slang; rugged, wide-open imagery.
    - Terms like: reckon, saddle up, long road, wanderin’.
    - Cadence has a slow, steady drawl.

• Southern:
    - Warm, melodic phrasing with elongated vowels.
    - Idioms like: y’all, fixin’ to, ain’t no.
    - Hospitality + grit; gentle lyrical rhythm.

• Appalachian:
    - Folk-rooted, old-mountain vocabulary; simple but poetic.
    - Words like: holler, lonesome, timberline, coal dust.
    - Cadence resembles ballads; sturdy and rhythmic.

• Celtic:
    - Lyrical, mythic phrasing with historic resonance.
    - Vocabulary: wanderer, stone, meadow, ancient, tide, moss.
    - Cadence resembles traditional Celtic songs: rolling, melodic.

#2.
GENRE determines structure, thematic focus, imagery style, and the musical "feel" of the lyrics. 
Each genre has its own emotional framing and traditional conventions.
DO NOT MENTION GENRE ATTRIBUTES UNLESS PROMPTED SPECIFICALLY

Genre Definitions:

• Pop:
    - Clean, catchy structure with short memorable lines.
    - Bright, simple imagery; emotional clarity.
    - Themes of love, self-expression, longing, empowerment.
    - Repetition is natural.

• Country:
    - Narrative storytelling with strong sense of place.
    - Everyday objects, rural settings, personal relationships.
    - Imagery: dirt roads, headlights, kitchen tables, backyards.
    - Warm, literal themes mixed with heartache or gratitude.

• Blues:
    - Call-and-response feel, internal repetition.
    - Themes: hardship, grit, resilience, longing.
    - Imagery: smokey rooms, storm weather, late-night lament.
    - Emotional but grounded.

• Soul:
    - Emotional depth with smooth, flowing lines.
    - Themes of heartbreak, devotion, personal struggle.
    - Imagery often tied to heat, touch, or spiritual longing.
    - Lines feel musical even without rhyme.

• Hip-Hop:
    - Rhythm-forward phrasing, confidence, wordplay.
    - Themes: identity, struggle, triumph, swagger.
    - Imagery: cityscapes, motion, internal monologue.
    - Cadence and punchlines more important than rhyme scheme.

• Indie Rock:
    - Poetic, symbolic imagery with emotional ambiguity.
    - Themes: introspection, bittersweet memory, escapism.
    - Longer lines; metaphor-heavy; atmospheric references.

• Folk:
    - Earthy, grounded storytelling; simple but resonant.
    - Imagery: rivers, seasons, family, footsteps, the natural world.
    - Themes often reflective, moral, or nostalgic.

• Bluegrass:
    - Fast-paced, rhythmic storytelling rooted in tradition.
    - Imagery: mountains, trains, labor, fiddle-driven energy.
    - Themes often center on hardship, motion, and heritage.

#3.
TONE determines the emotional atmosphere of the lyrics. 
Tone does NOT alter vocabulary or structure - only the emotional lens.
DO NOT MENTION TONE ATTRIBUTES UNLESS PROMPTED SPECIFICALLY

Tone Definitions:

• Sad:
    - Heavy emotional weight, longing, or loss.
    - Language feels slower, softer, reflective.

• Eerie:
    - Unsettling imagery; shadows, silence, unease.
    - Lines feel spacious or foreboding.

• Nostalgic:
    - Memory-driven warmth with bittersweet edges.
    - Past-focused imagery, longing for what was.

• Hopeful:
    - Upward emotional movement; small triumphs, new beginnings.
    - Bright light metaphors, motion, healing.

• Happy:
    - Lighthearted, energetic, full of color.
    - Affirmative phrases and uplifting moments.

##4.
Diction Behavior Rules (override all general writing habits):

If DICTION = “Conversational”:
    • Prioritize natural speech rhythms, short clauses, and realistic phrasing.
    • Use loose internal slant rhymes ONLY if they feel natural.
    • Do NOT prioritize rhyme schemes.
    • Cadence must feel spoken, not poetic or lyrical.
    • Flow > rhyme.

If DICTION = “Storytelling”:
    • Use clear narrative progression and temporal markers.
    • Moderate rhyme, mostly at line ends.
    • Maintain clarity over metaphor.

If DICTION = “Intimate”:
    • Use emotional closeness, sensory detail, and vulnerability.
    • Soft, subtle rhyme and gentle cadence.
    • Avoid big abstract metaphors - stay personal.

If DICTION = “Poetic”:
    • Prioritize imagery, metaphor, symbolism, and emotional layering.
    • Allow broken meter, enjambment, and textured language.
    • Rhyme optional but used meaningfully, not formulaically.

If DICTION = “Abstract”:
    • NO rhyme priority.
    • NO need for traditional line structure.
    • Prioritize conceptual contrast, surreal imagery, unexpected connections.
    • Flow is secondary; meaning density and conceptual layering come first.
